This week’s questions are about when to get your baby on a sleep schedule and whether you’re hurting your baby by sitting them up.

Our pediatrician recommends getting our baby on a sleep schedule by four months (three months and change now). What evidence supports this recommendation?

There are two underlying principles that might inform this recommendation. One is that babies need a lot of sleep; when they do not get enough, it’s a problem. And for babies and kids, the longest sleep should be at night.

The other principle is that sleep schedules tend to help parents sleep better and function better. If you know that your child is going to go to bed at 7 p.m., it’s a lot easier to plan and a lot easier to get the sleep that you need.

Having said this: if your child is getting enough sleep and you are happy with how things are going, then there isn’t anything in the data to say that you have to change it.

My mom keeps telling me I sit up my almost 4-month-old too much and that I’m hurting her bones and development. What’s the truth?

You are reaching a wonderful stage with your infant. That early cycle of eating, sleeping, and diaper changes is starting to shift, and now you’re getting real awake time to play, interact, and try new things together.

There is no evidence or concern that short sessions of sitting your 3–4 month-old upright, with support, cause any harm to their bones or development. These short, supported sessions, even done often, are not a concern.

Claims that supported sitting harms babies are not backed by any evidence.
