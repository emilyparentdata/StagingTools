Why Is Everyone Afraid of Sleep Training?

Addressing panic headlines and deciding on an approach

Welcome to the Free Digest, ParentData's weekly newsletter for free subscribers.

INTRO TEXT HERE

Every few weeks, someone on Instagram shares a reel or post with me on the evils of sleep training. Many of these posts are quite alarming, and the messages I get range from confused to panicked. I have been asked, much more than once, a form of Did I ruin my baby?

There has been no significant new data on sleep training in the last several years. The data remains very reassuring. However, it feels like some of the noise is getting louder, so I wanted to take this time to talk about why we’re hearing such scary things when the data doesn’t support them.

When I talk about “sleep training,” I mean any form of behavioral sleep intervention that involves some form of leaving the baby alone to fall asleep, with the possibility of some crying. 

This can include systems where the parents stay in the room, systems where they leave, and various forms where people leave and return. These approaches almost always include an organized bedtime routine. If you’re looking for more concrete advice about what type of sleep training to use, we’ve got that here.

Data on sleep training

My goal here is not to deliver a full discussion of the data on sleep training. If you want to read the full rundown with references, please find it here — the studies I reference in this article are discussed in much more detail there. To summarize briefly, here’s what we see from the data.

  • Sleep training increases infant sleep on average and reduces night wakings.
  • Sleep training reduces maternal depression and improves overall parental mental health.
  • Babies who are sleep trained do not show differences in mood or development either in the short term or in longer-term follow-up through early childhood. 
All of these findings are based on randomized controlled trials. To be clear, sleep training isn’t a panacea, and it works better for some babies than others, but the overall effects are positive. .

Why do I hear so many scary things? 

If the evidence doesn’t support these claims about risk, what are the scary claims in the media based on?

Let’s start with what they are not based on, which is any concrete evidence of harm from sleep training. These claims can make it seem like we have a litany of evidence showing that some kids are clearly damaged by sleep training. This is not true. As noted above, when we look at randomized studies of sleep training, we see (if anything) the opposite effect. 

Instead, the concerns are based on three theoretical or conceptual arguments.

First, we know that when children are significantly neglected (in Romanian orphanages or in parenting situations with abuse and neglect), this has long-term consequences for many things, including the children’s ability to form attachments. The argument is that sleep training is like this neglect and maybe has similar consequences. The issue with this argument is one of degree; cry-it-out sleep training with a baby who is loved and cared for is just completely different from this overall neglect. 

Second, there is an argument based on cortisol. One study showed that when babies cry during sleep training, their cortisol is elevated, and this elevation continues for at least one night after they sleep better. The concern is that perhaps these babies are then always exposed to very high cortisol levels, which could, in principle, be bad. However, we have no reason to think that the high levels of cortisol would continue and, perhaps more importantly, no way to compare them to overall cortisol levels in general. Moreover, if this were an issue, we would, again, expect to see it show up in some outcomes for children. Which it does not.

Finally, there is the evolutionary argument. For the length of human history, families mostly slept together, and the idea of sleeping separately, including sleep training, is new to modern times. This is true! But, again, it’s not clear what to take from this. We also have vaccines, sanitation, antibiotics, and an infant mortality rate well below 50%. Modern life differs in many ways and, as a result, may require different approaches.
